Butterflies are a type of insect that often fly during the day. In biological taxonomy, they belong to the superfamily Papilionoidea (scientific name: Papilionoidea, also known as the true butterfly superfamily) within the order Lepidoptera. There are a total of 7 families under it, including approximately 18,800 species. Like other insects, butterflies also belong to the arthropods. Their bodies have segmented exoskeletons. The adult's body is divided into three parts: head, thorax and abdomen. There are two pairs of wings on the thorax, and the various colors and patterns on the wings are composed of scales on the wings. Butterflies are holometabolous insects, and their life cycle consists of four stages: egg, larva, pupa and adult. The largest butterfly in the world is the Alexander swallowtail butterfly, with a wingspan of up to 280 millimeters. The smallest butterfly is the brown Grey butterfly, which is only 16 millimeters.
